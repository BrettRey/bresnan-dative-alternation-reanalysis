# Reviewer 3 — Acceptability-measurement referee

**Paper:** Brett Reynolds, "Production probability, transport, and grammatical possibility: Reanalysing the English dative alternation with open data."

**Reviewer standpoint:** experimental syntax and the empirical base of judgments (Sprouse on gradient acceptability and its task-dependence; Schütze on what judgments are evidence of; a Hay-style usage-based sensibility about keeping production and judgment as distinct instruments).

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

A clean, honestly-scoped reanalysis that reproduces Bresnan et al.'s dative production profile and shows it partly transports to BNC2014, then leans on acceptability evidence to do the conceptual work of separating production probability from grammatical possibility, while never actually putting an acceptability instrument in the room.

## 2. Strengths

1. **The level discipline is real and consistently held.** The paper does not let production frequency masquerade as grammaticality, and it says so repeatedly and precisely: production probability is "evidence about grammatical possibility, not a synonym for it" (sec. 7), the `languageR` and BNC2014 results "both concern production choice" (sec. 6), and the opportunity-set framing (sec. 6, Fig. opportunity-sets) makes the narrow denominator explicit ("given that a token enters the dative-alternation sample, which realization appears?", sec. 3). This is exactly the discipline a Hay/Sprouse referee wants to see, and most usage papers fail it. Here it is the spine of the paper.

2. **The transport test is the genuine empirical contribution and it is well-instrumented.** The scrambled-label control (log loss collapses from 0.308 to 0.885; AUC from 0.906 to 0.515, Table bnc-transport) is the right falsification design, and the paper resists the obvious overclaim by flagging that failure would not have been diagnostic because "variety, date, register, verb restriction, and annotation all move together" (sec. 5). Reporting the native-holdout gap (0.202 vs 0.308 log loss) as a "bounded downgrade rather than an apology" is the correct posture.

3. **DAIS is handled with unusual integrity.** The paper cites DAIS as a *future* bridge, states plainly that it is not analyzed because the repository has no explicit licence file and because "the outcome is judgement preference rather than corpus choice" (sec. 5), and the source-verification note documents the licence check (no `LICENSE`/`COPYING`, no GitHub licence API record, checked 2026-06-23). This is honest. A weaker paper would have scraped the 50K judgments and called it an acceptability analysis. I want to commend the restraint before I criticize what it leaves undone.

## 3. Weaknesses

### 3.1 The production/acceptability *divergence* is asserted, never demonstrated (most damaging)

This is the central problem. The paper's load-bearing claim is that acceptability evidence can cross a boundary that production frequency cannot: "Crossing that boundary needs acceptability evidence, not more tokens" (sec. 7); production data bears on grammaticality "only through an explicit bridge from attested choices to possible alternatives" (sec. 6). But no judgment datum appears anywhere in the paper. The argument that the two instruments *come apart* — that there exist dative configurations rated acceptable yet (near-)unattested, or attested yet degraded — is exactly the kind of claim that needs at least one worked case, and DAIS is sitting right there as the obvious source for it. As written, sec. 6's claim that "corpus probabilities can reveal structure that informal judgements miss" (sec. 6, last paragraph) is stated in the *opposite* direction from the boundary argument (there corpus beats judgment; in sec. 7 judgment is needed to beat corpus) and neither direction is shown with data. The result is an IOU: the structural-blindness argument tells you *where* a bridge would have to be built (Fig. opportunity-sets is literally captioned to that effect) without building any span of it. **Actionable:** either (a) reframe explicitly as a programmatic/position piece whose empirical contribution is the transport result and whose acceptability claim is a stated next step — and drop the implication that the boundary has been *shown* to be crossable — or (b) license DAIS (email the authors; CC-BY-equivalent permission is cheap to obtain) and demonstrate one divergence case. Without one of these, the title's third noun ("grammatical possibility") is carried entirely by argument, not evidence.

### 3.2 An unstated premise that acceptability ≈ grammaticality more directly than production does

The target/channel framing (sec. 6) treats corpus choices and judgments as "two channels" bearing "indirectly" on grammaticality as "target." Good — symmetric and honest so far. But the conclusion is *not* symmetric: production is "silent by construction" about the unattested-but-grammatical region (sec. 6, sec. 7), whereas acceptability is nominated as the instrument that can reach it. That asymmetry needs an argument the paper does not give. Why should a graded acceptability judgment detect "grammatical-but-unattested" any better than frequency? A Sprouse-style objection: judgments are themselves gradient, noisy, and task-dependent — magnitude estimation, forced choice, and Likert can disagree; ratings are pulled by frequency, plausibility, priming, and processing load. The paper *names* this once — "a judgement task samples a noisy, role-dependent instrument" (sec. 6) — and then proceeds to trust that same instrument as the boundary-crossing channel without saying why its noise is the *benign* kind. A low rating can mean ungrammatical, or rare, or hard to process, or pragmatically odd out of context — the very confound the paper correctly attributes to *informal* judgments in sec. 2 ("a construction may sound odd because it's impossible, because it is rare, because the wrong verb is present..."). That confound does not disappear when judgments are collected at scale; DAIS aggregates 50K judgments but aggregation reduces variance, not the systematic frequency/plausibility contamination. **Actionable:** add a paragraph in sec. 6 that does for acceptability what sec. 2 does for production — name what a graded judgment *cannot* by itself distinguish — so the two channels are treated as symmetrically fallible. Then state the actual reason acceptability can reach the unattested region (presumably: you can *construct* and rate a stimulus that no corpus contains; you cannot observe a token that did not occur). That last point is the real argument and it is currently implicit.

### 3.3 The target/channel metaphor risks hiding the measurement problem it claims to solve

"Grammaticality is the target those channels bear on indirectly" (sec. 6) is clean prose, but as a *measurement* claim it is underspecified. If grammaticality is a latent target and both production and acceptability are noisy indicators of it, the natural move is a measurement model that estimates the latent from both channels and quantifies how much each contributes — and how they diverge. The paper gestures at this structure (channels, target, indirect bearing) without committing to it operationally, so "target" does duty as a metaphor for "the thing we cannot measure directly" rather than as a defined estimand. Reviewer 3's worry: the framing *names* the measurement problem (production and acceptability are different instruments) and then resolves it rhetorically rather than measuredly. The opportunity-set figure is the strongest part of this section precisely because it is concrete about denominators; the target/channel paragraph is the weakest because it is not. **Actionable:** either define grammaticality here as an estimand with a stated (even if not-yet-fitted) measurement model linking the two channels, or downgrade "target/channel" to an admitted heuristic and let the opportunity-set / denominator argument carry the section. Do not let a tidy three-part abstraction ("one channel... another channel... the target") stand in for the divergence demonstration that 3.1 is asking for.

### Minor

- Sec. 5 ends and sec. 6 opens both citing DAIS / the bridge; the two passages overlap and could be consolidated so the licence caveat is stated once.
- Sec. 6 says corpus probabilities "can reveal structure that informal judgements miss," but the paper's framework distinguishes *informal* judgments (sec. 2) from *controlled* acceptability tasks (sec. 6). The conclusion's "acceptability evidence" should specify *controlled* acceptability, or a reader will hear sec. 6 and sec. 7 as contradicting each other on whether judgments help or hurt.

## 4. Key question at Q&A

You argue acceptability evidence can reach the grammatical-but-unattested region that production cannot. Give me one concrete dative configuration where you predict a *divergence* — high acceptability, near-zero corpus probability, or the reverse — and tell me how a graded DAIS-style rating distinguishes "grammatical but dispreferred" from "low-rated because rare/implausible/hard-to-process," given that those are precisely the confounds you (correctly) warn against for informal judgments in section 2. If the rating cannot make that cut, what makes acceptability a *better* channel to the target than frequency, rather than just a *different* one?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The reproduction and transport work is solid and honestly scoped, but the paper's titular third claim (grammatical possibility, reachable via acceptability) is currently an unredeemed IOU: no judgment data is analyzed, the production/acceptability divergence is asserted rather than shown, and acceptability is quietly trusted as the boundary-crossing channel without confronting its own task-dependence. Redeem it with one worked DAIS divergence case, or reframe explicitly as a position piece whose empirical core is transport and whose acceptability bridge is declared future work.
