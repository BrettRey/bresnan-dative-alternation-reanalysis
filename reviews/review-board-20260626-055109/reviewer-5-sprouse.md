### 1. One-sentence summary

The paper reframes cross-corpus transfer of a Bresnan-style dative production model as a full predictive-evaluation problem (proper scoring, calibration, a same-row target-native benchmark, predictor ablation, missingness, and an explicit opportunity-set denominator), concluding that the non-lexical conditioning profile transports within a high-frequency core while staying silent, by construction, about grammaticality, with a supplementary DAIS bridge showing production and acceptability as distinct channels.

### 2. Strengths

- **Explanatory-level discipline is exactly right.** The paper refuses to equate a fitted production probability with grammaticality, and treats the DAIS judgement channel as a different target rather than a confirmation of the production model (section 06, lines 38-43; supplementary, lines 9-24). This is the correct stance: corpus production and acceptability are different data types, and most corpus papers blur them. Keeping them apart is the paper's best methodological move.

- **The judgement-level model has the right skeleton and is honestly reported.** Section 08 (lines 70-79) fits crossed random intercepts for participant and item, puts the manipulated conditions (recipient condition, theme type, verb) in as fixed effects, and then reports that the *incremental* production-score coefficient is essentially zero (0.017, SE 0.076). Many authors would have stopped at the marginal r = 0.667 and declared the model "validated against judgements." This one explicitly does not, and says the correlation is largely shared sensitivity to the manipulated predictors.

- **The calibration logic in the BNC2014 section is genuinely careful** (section 05, lines 158-167): it separates a prevalence/level shift from residual structure by showing intercept-and-slope recalibration does not close the gap. That is the correct template.

### 3. Weaknesses

- **The headline DAIS divergence (`offer`) is selected on the raw difference, not on calibration-adjusted residuals, so it confounds low production probability with genuine channel separation.** The paper applies its own slope-vs-residual discipline to BNC2014 but not to DAIS. Run that move on Table 4: regress DAIS DO preference on production probability across the six verb means. I get slope ≈ 0.21, intercept ≈ 0.38, and residuals of roughly `give` −0.02, `lend` 0.00, `offer` +0.06, `sell` −0.08, `send` +0.01, `show` +0.03. After calibration, **`sell` is the larger outlier (and in the opposite direction, less DO-preferred than predicted), while `offer`'s residual is modest.** `Offer` looks "clearest" only because it has the lowest production probability and the DAIS means are compressed (0.35-0.52) relative to production (0.15-0.51), so the raw vertical gap is mechanically largest there. The single illustrative item ("Juan offered the woman a payment," DO pref 0.694) sits far above `offer`'s own mean of 0.468, so it overstates the verb effect. **Action:** report per-verb differences with bootstrap CIs, base any divergence claim on residuals after a calibration slope, and reconcile or retire the `offer` example, which currently propagates into section 06 and section 07 as the load-bearing illustration despite the "for completeness only" hedge in the supplementary.

- **Judgement-task effects are never separated from stable preference or calibration shift, because the DAIS response format is not described.** The supplementary calls the item mean a "human DO preference on a 0-1 scale" (line 23) but never says whether a judgement is a binary forced choice, a slider, or a Likert rating. This matters two ways. First, if judgements are binary forced choices (10 per pair, consistent with the 0-1 item means), the "linear mixed model on 1,525 judgements" (line 71) is a linear-probability model and a logistic GLMM is the standard choice; either way the link should be stated and justified. Second, the −0.157 mean offset and the central compression of the DAIS verb means (0.35-0.52) are exactly what aggregated forced-choice or rating data produce by central tendency, independent of any production/preference dissociation. **Action:** state the task, justify the model link, and show the offset survives a task-compression null (e.g., does it persist after rescaling DAIS to the production range, or in a model that allows a DAIS slope < 1?).

- **Item-mean and participant uncertainty never reach the headline statistics, so neither stable preference nor structured heterogeneity is established.** The r = 0.667 / ρ = 0.685 are computed over 150 item means, each from ~10 judgements, treated as fixed points; no split-half or test-retest reliability of the item means is reported, so the attenuation ceiling on "moderate correspondence" is unknown. On the participant side, 1,525 judgements over 923 participants is ~1.65 judgements/participant in this subset, which makes the participant random intercept barely identifiable and folds any structured between-speaker variation (the dative shows real lectal variation) into noise. Relatedly, scoring a model fit on naturalistic Switchboard covariate distributions onto DAIS's balanced factorial stimuli is an out-of-domain extrapolation into sparse length/definiteness cells, a caveat the paper raises for BNC2014 but not for DAIS. **Action:** report item-mean reliability, propagate it (or use item-level CIs / a measurement-error model) into the correlation, and flag the factorial-stimulus extrapolation explicitly.

### 4. Key question for Q&A

You separate a calibration/prevalence shift from residual structure for BNC2014 but not for DAIS. If you regress DO preference on production probability across the six verbs and inspect residuals, is `offer` still the standout, or is `sell` the larger (opposite-signed) outlier, and once you also allow for forced-choice central-tendency compression in the ratings, what is left of the "channels separate, most clearly for `offer`" claim that section 06 and section 07 rely on?

### 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The framing, the external-validation ladder, and the level discipline are sound and worth publishing, but the supplementary's `offer` divergence (which leaks into the main text twice) is not robust to the paper's own calibration logic and does not separate task and compression effects from genuine preference, all fixable without new data.
