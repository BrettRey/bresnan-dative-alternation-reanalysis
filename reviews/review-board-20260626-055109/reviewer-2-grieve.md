# Reviewer 2 — corpus sociolinguistics / corpus comparability standpoint

## 1. One-sentence summary of the main claim

Freezing a Bresnan-style production model of the English dative (NP vs. PP recipient) and moving it from American `languageR` data to Spoken BNC2014 shows that the conditioning profile partly transports, that the portable part is mainly non-lexical, and that a target-native model is better calibrated than discrimination statistics alone would reveal; the contribution is the full validation regime (proper scoring, calibration, paired native benchmark, ablation, missingness, and an explicit opportunity-set denominator), not the finding that the dative travels.

## 2. Strengths

- **Estimand discipline is genuinely above the field norm.** Section 6 names the fitted quantity as P(NP recipient | a token entered the annotated NP/PP dative sample), and the nested-denominator figure (`fig:opportunity-sets`) refuses the slide from production probability to probability of grammaticality. The observational-identification point (no quantity of further positive tokens under the same design reaches the grammatical-but-unattested region) is correct and rarely stated this cleanly in corpus work.
- **It refuses the population claim where most papers would make it.** The metadata audit in §5 (lines 91–98) and the explicit instruction that "a single BNC2014 transport score should therefore be read as a corpus-domain diagnostic, not as a clean estimate for British spoken English in general" is exactly the right move. This is the distinction I most often have to force on corpus papers, and here it is volunteered.
- **Exemplary candor about the missing identifiers.** §5 (lines 100–108) and §6 (lines 85–93) state plainly that no stable speaker/conversation ID survives in the released 44-column CSV, that the native benchmark is therefore row-level CV, and that the row bootstrap and Wilson intervals are "conditional row-level intervals ... which probably makes them too narrow." Most authors would bury this; foregrounding it is the right call.

## 3. Weaknesses (actionable)

- **Register/genre comparability is the largest uncontrolled confound and is under-weighted relative to "variety."** The source spoken data is Switchboard: telephone talk between paired strangers on assigned topics (verify against the Switchboard documentation, `GodfreyHolliman1993Switchboard1`). The BNC2014 target is face-to-face spontaneous talk weighted toward intimates (close-family, partner, and very-close-friend conversations are 1,171 of 1,839 rows, §5 line 94). "Conversation" is not one register across these corpora. Yet the paper repeatedly frames the test as American → British (§5 lines 42–47), which invites a dialect reading the bundled design cannot support, while register appears only once, as one word in the bundling list (conclusion, line 17). Actionable: foreground the telephone-stranger vs. face-to-face-intimate contrast as a first-class confound, soften the variety framing toward a corpus-to-corpus framing, and name the comparison corpus that would actually unbundle variety from register and period (e.g., a spoken AmE 2010s set, or a spoken BrE 1990s set). Relatedly, representativeness claims about Spoken BNC2014 currently lean on the author's own audit of the dative file and on the derived-dataset papers (`JensetMcGillivray2018BNCData`, `JensetMcGillivray2019Dative`); they should cite the corpus-design/sampling documentation (Love et al. 2017, *The Spoken BNC2014* — flag: verify this reference, I have not opened it in this workspace).
- **Row-level social metadata is audited but never used or connected to the outcome.** §5 reports gender (1,138 female-coded, 699 male-coded) and age (modal band 19–29, 736 rows) per row, then drops them. If those fields are row-level in the released CSV, the skew is testable: does the BNC2014 NP rate vary by age band or gender? Either run that check (descriptive at minimum, with the pseudoreplication caveat flagged, since there are no speaker IDs), or state explicitly that social conditioning of the dative is untested here and argue why the demographic skew is or is not a threat to the transport comparison. As written, the audit describes a skew and does nothing with it, which is exactly the gap a corpus-sociolinguistics reviewer will press.
- **The transport-specific lexical claim rests on six verbs and sits in tension with the `offer` result.** The headline "the portable part is mainly non-lexical rather than lexical base rates" (abstract; §5 lines 137–147; conclusion) is a cross-corpus claim drawn from a six-verb core, where adding verb terms changes log loss by only 0.012 with an interval crossing zero ([-0.006, 0.030]). Two things complicate it. First, the harmonized transport model already omits recipient definiteness (§5 lines 65–66), one of the stronger Bresnan predictors, so "the model transported" is really "a reduced harmonized model transported"; the abstract's "freeze a production model fit to the `languageR` dative data" risks implying the full model went across intact. Second, the supplementary DAIS bridge shows `offer` diverging by -0.319 (`tab:dais-bridge`; §8 lines 81–87), which is a lexically specific cross-context effect, the opposite of "lexical doesn't matter much." Actionable: scope the lexical/non-lexical transport claim explicitly to the six high-frequency verbs, note the absent recipient-definiteness predictor where the claim is made, and reconcile the `offer` divergence with the "non-lexical travels" framing.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

Switchboard is topic-prompted telephone talk between strangers; the Spoken BNC2014 dative rows are predominantly face-to-face talk among intimates. Variety, period, and register are bundled, and you show the residual calibration gap survives an intercept-and-slope recalibration. So what is the evidence that the residual gap is about American-versus-British grammar at all, rather than telephone-stranger versus face-to-face-intimate register? And given that, would you reframe the headline as corpus-to-corpus transport rather than AmE → BrE?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** Methodologically exemplary on estimand and missing-data honesty, but the bundled design is framed too much as a variety (AmE → BrE) result when register is the larger uncontrolled confound, and row-level social metadata is audited without being used or connected to the outcome.
