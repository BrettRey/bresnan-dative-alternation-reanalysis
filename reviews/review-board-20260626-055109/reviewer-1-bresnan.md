# Review — Reviewer 1 (Joan Bresnan standpoint)

*Source-grounding note: where I characterize specific prior papers (Bresnan 2007 rating study, Bresnan & Ford 2010, Röthlisberger et al. 2017, and the clinical-prediction external-validation literature), I am working from my own knowledge, not from sources read in this workspace. Each such claim is flagged "[verify]" and the authors should check it against the actual papers before relying on it.*

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper freezes a Bresnan et al. (2007) dative production model and evaluates its transport to Spoken BNC2014 as a full predictive-validation problem (proper scoring, calibration, same-row native benchmark, ablation, missingness, denominator), arguing that the validated quantity is conditional production choice within an attested NP/PP frame and is therefore silent about grammatical possibility by construction.

## 2. Strengths

- **The estimand discipline is genuinely excellent and rare in this literature.** The explicit statement P(NP recipient | a token entered the annotated NP/PP dative sample) in §6, together with the nested-opportunity-set figure (Fig. ref `opportunity-sets`), blocks the slide from corpus frequency to grammaticality that probabilistic-grammar work is often accused of. The observational-identification claim (more positive tokens under the same design D₀ cannot reach the grammatical-but-unattested cells) is correctly reasoned and well stated.
- **The same-row paired native benchmark plus calibration is a real advance over a lone concordance index.** Table `bnc-transport` scores source-trained and target-native predictions on the identical 1,621 rows, and the finding that intercept-and-slope recalibration does not close the gap ("not only a prevalence shift," §5/§6) is non-obvious and honestly reported. Discrimination-only evaluation, as in the prior transfer studies, would have hidden it.
- **The honesty about data limits is exemplary.** The missing-rows-are-outcome-skewed audit (NP rate 0.819 complete vs. 0.670 incomplete, §5), the disclosure that the released BNC2014 file dropped the speaker/conversation IDs so folds can only be row-level, and the explicit statement that the reported intervals are therefore "probably too narrow" (§6) are exactly the disclosures most papers omit.

## 3. Weaknesses (actionable)

**W1. The regime validates that *predictions* transport, but not that the *grammar* transports — and the grammar is the linguistically meaningful object.** Bresnan et al.'s contribution was that the soft constraints (animacy, definiteness, givenness, pronominality, harmonic alignment) are gradient grammatical knowledge, not just performance preferences. Your ablation (§5: marginal, verb-only, non-verb, full core) stops at the verb/non-verb boundary and never opens the "non-verb" bundle. So the paper reports that an aggregate non-lexical profile transports without reporting whether any individual constraint keeps its sign and relative magnitude in BNC2014. Two models can share an AUC with reshuffled or sign-flipped constraints. **Action:** report constraint-level transport directly. You already fit a native BNC2014 model (Table `bnc-transport`); print its coefficients beside the source coefficients and say whether animacy, definiteness, pronominality, and length retain direction and rough strength. That is the bridge between "prediction" and "grammar," and right now it is missing. Relatedly, the calibration miss you find (source underpredicts NP for *send*, *offer*, *lend*; overpredicts for *sell*) is a clue to *which* constraints differ across the 1990s-American/2010s-British gap, but it is left as a generic "local structure" residual rather than followed to a linguistic source.

**W2. The prior-work foil is too flat, precisely on the grammar/usage bridge.** §5 lumps Bresnan & Ford (2010) and Röthlisberger, Grafmiller & Szmrecsanyi (2017) as documenting "broad cross-variety stability by other means" and characterizes the transfer literature as reporting only "discrimination." That undersells them on the dimension this paper cares about. Bresnan & Ford (2010) connected corpus probability to processing and rating across American/Australian varieties [verify], and Röthlisberger et al. (2017) fit variety-native models and compared constraint stability [verify], which is much closer to your "target-native benchmark" and constraint-transport question than "stability by other means" admits. The DAIS bridge compounds this: it is framed as discovering production/acceptability separation, but the original program's experimental claim (Bresnan 2007 [verify]) was convergence-with-mismatch between corpus probability and human ratings, so channel separation is not a new discovery. Separately, the validation ladder itself (calibration-in-the-large + slope, recalibration, model updating) is the standard external-validation framework from clinical prediction modelling (Steyerberg, Van Calster, TRIPOD/Collins) [verify], and the text credits no source for it. **Action:** re-characterize these works precisely, and position the contribution as *importing and assembling* that external-validation framework for probabilistic grammar (a legitimate contribution) rather than as inventing a regime. Cite the source tradition for calibration intercept/slope.

**W3. The DAIS bridge's central number challenges the Bresnan convergence thesis more than the supplement admits, and it is doing load-bearing work in the main text.** §6 leans on the *offer* divergence to motivate the grammaticality boundary, and the conclusion (§7) cites it again, yet the supplement (§8) shows the incremental production-score coefficient is essentially zero once condition, theme type, and verb are represented (item-level slope 0.006, SE 0.078; judgement-level 0.017, SE 0.076, interval [-0.132, 0.165]). For a Bresnan-aligned reader, "corpus probability adds near nothing to acceptability beyond the design factors" is the most pointed result in the paper, and it sits in a supplement under the generous heading "the two channels line up moderately." **Action:** decide what the bridge is. If it is peripheral, stop leaning on *offer* twice in the main text. If it is central, promote it and address power (150 items cannot distinguish "adds nothing" from "adds a little"). Note also the free win you are missing: the *offer* anomaly appears in *both* channels (source underpredicts NP in BNC2014 production *and* underpredicts DO acceptance in DAIS), a coherent cross-channel signal that the 1990s-American model has *offer*'s bias wrong. Connecting those would add the linguistic payload W1 asks for.

*Minor:* the title ("External validation of probabilistic grammar") promises more than the body delivers, since the body validates a production-choice predictor and then declares grammar out of scope. Consider "production model" or "production-choice profile" in the title to match the scope discipline you enforce everywhere else.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

You have shown that *predictions* transport. Does the *grammar* transport? Concretely: when you fit the native BNC2014 model, do the soft-constraint coefficients (recipient/theme animacy, definiteness, givenness, pronominality, length) keep the signs and relative magnitudes Bresnan et al. reported? If they do, that is the strongest result in the paper and it is currently invisible; if they do not, isn't *that* the linguistically meaningful transport finding, rather than the aggregate calibration gap?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** A careful, honest, well-scoped methodological paper whose validity I do not doubt; the revisions needed (constraint-level transport, accurate crediting of the Bresnan program and the prediction-validation tradition, and resolving the DAIS bridge's status) add linguistic payload and fix positioning without requiring any new data.
