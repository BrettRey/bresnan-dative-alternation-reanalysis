## Reviewer 6 — Projectibility and contribution scepticism

### 1. One-sentence summary of the main claim

When cross-corpus transport of a Bresnan-style dative production model is treated as a full predictive-evaluation problem (proper scoring, calibration, a same-row target-native benchmark, predictor ablation, and an explicit opportunity-set denominator), the frozen source profile discriminates well out of domain but stays measurably worse-calibrated than a target-native fit, and the fitted quantity is a conditional production probability that projects within a stated domain while remaining silent, by construction, about the grammatical-but-unattested region.

### 2. Strengths

- **The estimand and opportunity-set discipline is the paper's best feature and is genuinely rare in corpus linguistics.** Stating the fitted quantity as P(NP recipient | a token entered the annotated NP/PP sample) (section 06, lines 4-13) and refusing to call it P(grammatical) is exactly the level-discipline this literature usually skips. The nested-denominator figure and the observational-identification claim (more positive tokens under the same design D₀ cannot identify the empty region) give projectibility real boundary-drawing work.
- **The paired same-row native benchmark is a real methodological improvement over prior transfer studies.** Scoring the source-trained and BNC2014-native out-of-fold predictions on the identical 1,621 complete-case rows (Table 2, section 05) is the right design, and it is what lets the discrimination/calibration split surface (source full log loss 0.308 vs native 0.227; recalibration floor 0.290, still short of native). Prior work reporting only a concordance index could not have seen this.
- **Unusual honesty and reproducibility.** MD5 checksums, fixed seeds, the candid admission that the same-data reanalysis "does not create a new grammatical conclusion" (section 04, line 56), the warning that a single BNC2014 score is "a corpus-domain diagnostic, not a clean estimate for British spoken English in general" (section 05, lines 96-98), and the conditional-interval caveat about within-conversation dependence (section 06, lines 89-93). I would adopt the discrimination-vs-calibration rung distinction myself.

### 3. Weaknesses (specific and actionable)

- **The single most projectible finding is noticed but never cashed out.** Section 05 (lines 137-147) establishes that the portable part of the profile is non-lexical conditioning, while lexical verb base rates barely beat marginal (verb-only 0.456 vs marginal 0.473). This is the one result with genuine inductive reach, and it is left as a bare distributional fact. Projectibility, as your own keyword, should mean a forward prediction: do you expect the same non-lexical-transports/lexical-doesn't split for the genitive or particle-verb alternation, and if so under what pressure (e.g. processing-general constraints travel, variety-specific lexical biases do not)? Right now the conditioning profile is descriptive rather than projectible. Either state the candidate generalization as a testable claim or explicitly demote it to a corpus-pair-specific observation and drop the projectibility billing for it. (You correctly avoid mechanism-talk per level-discipline; the gap is that the most generalizable finding then has no hook to project from.)
- **The contribution is framed as a seven-item checklist, and the novel rungs are not separated from the standard ones.** Table 2 (validation ladder) mixes bread-and-butter predictive-validation tools (proper scoring, calibration intercept/slope, ablation, all routine in clinical prediction modelling) with the two rungs that are actually new to *probabilistic grammar transport*: the opportunity-set audit and the same-row paired native benchmark. As written, a reader cannot tell what you are importing from what you are contributing. Foreground the one or two genuinely new rungs. Relatedly, the opportunity-set argument restates, in identification language, the variationist "envelope of variation" / principle of accountability (Labov, Wolfram, the tradition Bresnan's own work sits in). I am flagging this from background knowledge, not a source read in this workspace, so verify the exact framing, but the paper needs to engage that prior art so its novelty (the D₀ non-identification claim) is visible rather than reading as a renamed truism.
- **The ladder over-projects itself from n = 1, and the one result that would independently support your central claim is buried.** Section 06 (line 79) asserts "the ladder transfers to other alternations under stated preconditions," but it has been run on exactly one source, one target, one alternation, six verbs. The paper warns against projecting beyond the evidence and then does so for its own method. Either demonstrate the ladder once more (a second corpus pair, or the genitive alternation on existing open data) or downgrade the transfer claim to a stated conjecture with a test. And note that the DAIS near-null (section 08: incremental production-score slope 0.006, SE 0.078 at item level; 0.017, SE 0.076 in the mixed model) is not merely "moderate alignment", it is direct evidence that production probability and judgement are different targets, which is precisely your section-06 identification claim. That confirmation is currently sequestered in supplementary material and framed as a correlation story rather than as support for the main argument.

### 4. Key question for Q&A

You find that non-lexical conditioning transports but lexical verb base rates do not (section 05). Is that a projectible generalization, that is, would you predict the same split before fitting on a *different* alternation such as the genitive, and what would license that prediction? If you would not predict it, then what does the finding license beyond this one American-to-British corpus pair, and in what sense is the result "projectible" rather than a one-off description?

### 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The estimand discipline and the same-row native benchmark are real contributions and the paper is honest and reproducible, but it draws projectibility's boundary without ever exercising its forward payoff, and it projects its own validation ladder from a single case in exactly the way it cautions others against.
