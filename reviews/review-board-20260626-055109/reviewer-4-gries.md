# Reviewer 4 (corpus-methods / alternation-studies standpoint)

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper reframes the cross-corpus transport of a Bresnan-style dative production model as a full predictive-evaluation problem (proper scoring, calibration, a paired target-native benchmark on identical rows, predictor-group ablation, missing-data sensitivity, and an explicit opportunity-set denominator), and shows that the frozen `languageR` profile discriminates well on Spoken BNC2014 but stays worse-calibrated than a target-native fit, with the portable part being mainly non-lexical conditioning rather than lexical base rates.

## 2. Strengths

- **The estimand discipline is the right corrective, and it is rare.** Making the denominator explicit (Sec. 6, Fig. `opportunity-sets-nested`) and insisting the fitted quantity is `P(NP | token entered the annotated NP/PP sample)` rather than a probability of grammaticality is exactly the move alternation studies usually skip. Pairing it with the calibration-vs-discrimination contrast (Table `bnc-transport`; the source full model's AUC rising to 0.906 while its log loss is *worse* than the non-verb model at 0.308 vs 0.296) is a genuinely useful demonstration that a concordance index hides.

- **Unusual honesty about the data ceiling.** The paper states plainly (Sec. 5, Sec. 6) that the Spoken BNC2014 release dropped the speaker and conversation identifiers, so only row-level CV is possible, within-conversation dependence is not removed, and the reported intervals are "probably too narrow." Most transport papers report a single C-index and stop; flagging the resampling ceiling as a property of the release rather than burying it is to the author's credit.

- **The ablation is the correct probe, and it is interpreted cautiously.** Decomposing transport into marginal / verb-only / non-verb / full (Table `bnc-transport`) and reading the verb-only gain over marginal as small (0.456 vs 0.473) is the right way to ask "what travels," and the paper resists the easy story that shared verb base rates do the work.

## 3. Weaknesses

- **Denominator harmonization is asserted at the outcome level but never audited at the interchangeability level.** Sec. 5 says "the outcome maps cleanly: `VNN` becomes NP and `VNPP` becomes PP," and treats that as the hard part. But the alternation denominator is not the surface pattern; it is the set of tokens that *could* have gone either way. `languageR::dative` reflects Bresnan et al.'s inclusion/exclusion criteria (alternating tokens, with idiomatic and non-alternating cases handled their way); the Jenset & McGillivray BNC2014 extraction reflects a different project's criteria. If one admits `show him the door`-type fixed/non-alternating `VNN` tokens and the other does not, the two "alternation samples" are not the same kind of object, and part of the source-vs-native gap is a difference of denominators, not of grammar or domain. *Actionable:* add a crosswalk of the two extraction protocols, report how many tokens each interchangeability filter removes, and (ideally) re-run the headline comparison on a denominator reconciled to one set of criteria. I have not read the two dataset-documentation sources in this workspace, so the specific divergence is a hypothesis to verify, but the audit is owed regardless.

- **The "non-lexical travels better than lexical" conclusion is confounded with the six-verb selection, and the paper does not scope it accordingly.** The verb-only/non-verb contrast is computed only on `give, send, show, sell, offer, lend` (Sec. 5), which are the canonical free-alternating high-frequency core. Those are plausibly the verbs whose base rates are *most* similar across varieties; the peripheral verbs with strong, idiosyncratic, potentially non-transporting construction-verb associations (the collexemes that carry variety-specific signal) are excluded by the shared-verb restriction. So "lexical base rates don't transport" may partly be "I selected the lexical core where verb identity is least informative." The paper notes these are "the high-frequency core, not a random sample," but does not connect that caveat to the ablation interpretation. *Actionable:* scope the claim explicitly to the shared high-frequency core, and show sensitivity to verb-set composition (e.g., leave-one-verb-out on the six, or report whether the conclusion holds if the core is split by DO-bias). The six-verb verb-only AUC of 0.646 is on a truncated lexical range and should not be read as a general statement about lexical conditioning.

- **Predictor non-equivalence across annotation regimes is folded into "domain-specific structure," yet it is a rival explanation for the headline calibration gap.** Only length harmonization is examined carefully (character-count vs word-count, Sec. 5). Animacy and pronominality were coded by two different annotation projects under possibly different schemes (Bresnan's animacy was famously more granular than binary), and definiteness survives only as a regex proxy. A frozen coefficient applied to a same-named-but-differently-defined predictor will miscalibrate for reasons that have nothing to do with British vs American grammar. The native model, refit on BNC2014, automatically absorbs BNC2014's coding conventions; so its calibration advantage (log loss 0.227 vs 0.308; recalibration to 0.290 still short of 0.227) could be measuring annotation adaptation, not grammatical adaptation. *Actionable:* add a per-predictor crosswalk plus a distributional comparison (mean/range of recipient and theme length, animacy/pronominality marginals) across the two datasets, and name annotation incomparability as an explicit alternative to "domain-specific structure" when interpreting the residual gap.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

Your central contrast is that the target-native model is better calibrated than the frozen source model and that an intercept-and-slope recalibration does not close the gap. The native model is refit on BNC2014 while the source model is frozen, so it necessarily absorbs BNC2014's annotation scheme, its complete-case prevalence, and any within-conversation regularity that row-level CV cannot fence off. How much of the residual gap is a fact about British spoken grammar, versus the native model fitting BNC2014's coding conventions and clustered idiosyncrasies that the source model is structurally barred from seeing? What would convince you it is the former?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The framing and estimand discipline are sound and worth publishing, but the denominator-interchangeability audit, the six-verb scoping of the ablation, and a predictor-equivalence crosswalk all touch the headline finding directly and must be addressed before the transport claim is safe.
